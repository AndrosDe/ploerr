History

Originally Plörrbräu was only brewed on Castle Plörr.
It all began with Lord Otto von Rinnsal when he ventured on another plan on getting the best out of people:
Money!
To prevent people to fell more or less seriously ill from polluted water, he started a brewery to support the workers with beer. Due to the high ratio of Water is higher to barley and hops, it was called Dünnbräu.
This way fewer workers got sick, he could charge them for the beer and the fields got watered automatically.

Being one of the few still drinkable beers in the German "Biernot" crisis (caused by an ergot infestation of wheat and barley), it saved multiple festivals, like the Oktoberfest, from being sober events.
As thanks named the Duke of Bavaria him "Otto von Plörr"
and the Castle Rinnsal became Castle Plörr until it's foreclosure.
Therefore making Dünnbräu into Plörrbräu.

Nowadays Plörrbräu the new Brewhouse is in the old local mining building, which was used to store mining equipment and heavy mining machinery.
Since 1990 it is part of the German Cultural Heritage.

The Brewing

The purpose of brewing is to convert the starch source into a sugary liquid called wort and to convert the wort into an alcoholic drink known as beer in a fermentation process affected by yeast.
The first step, where the wort is prepared by mixing the starch source (normally malted barley) with hot water, is known as "mashing". Hot water is mixed with crushed malt in a mash tun.
The mashing process takes around 1 to 2 hours, during which the starches are converted to sugars, and then the sweet wort is drained off the grains.
The grains are then washed in a process known as "sparging". This washing allows the brewer to gather as much of the fermentable liquid from the grains as possible. The process of filtering the spent grain from the wort and sparge water is called wort separation.
The traditional process for wort separation is lautering, in which the grain bed itself serves as the filter medium.

This washing allows the brewer to gather as much of the fermentable liquid from the grains as possible. In the continuous sparge, collect the original wort and the sparge water together. The sweet wort collected from sparging is put into a kettle, or "copper" and boiled, usually for about one hour. During boiling, water in the wort evaporates, but the sugars and other components of the wort remain; this allows more efficient use of the starch sources in the beer. Boiling also destroys any remaining enzymes left over from the mashing stage. Hops are added during boiling as a source of bitterness, flavor, and aroma. Hops may be added at more than one point during the boil.

After boiling, the hopped wort is cooled and ready for the yeast. In some breweries, the hopped wort may pass through a hopback, which is a small vat filled with hops, to add aromatic hop flavoring and to act as a filter; but usually, the hopped wort is simply cooled for the fermenter, where the yeast is added. During fermentation, the wort becomes beer in a process that requires a week to months depending on the type of yeast and strength of the beer. Once fermentation is complete, the yeast also settles, leaving the beer clear.

During fermentation, most of the carbon dioxide is allowed to escape through a trap and the beer is left with carbonation of only about one atmosphere of pressure. The carbonation is often increased either by transferring the beer to a pressure vessel such as a keg and introducing pressurized carbon dioxide or by transferring it before the fermentation is finished so that carbon dioxide pressure builds up inside the container as the fermentation finishes
